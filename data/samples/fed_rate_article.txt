The United States Federal Reserve raised its benchmark interest rate by 0.25 percentage points on July 26, 2023, bringing the federal funds rate to a target range of 5.25% to 5.50% — the highest level in 22 years. The decision was unanimous among the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) members and marked the eleventh rate increase since the Fed began its aggressive tightening cycle in March 2022.

Fed Chair Jerome Powell, speaking at a press conference following the decision, said the central bank remained "data dependent" and did not rule out further rate increases if inflation failed to return to its 2% target. Annual inflation, as measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI), stood at 3.0% in June 2023, down sharply from its peak of 9.1% in June 2022, but still above the Fed's target.

The rate hike was intended to further cool the U.S. economy and suppress inflation, which had been running at multi-decade highs driven by supply chain disruptions, pandemic-era stimulus spending, and the energy price shock following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022.

Economists and markets reacted cautiously to the announcement. Futures markets at the time of the decision priced in approximately a 27% probability of another rate hike in September 2023. Several investment banks, including Goldman Sachs, forecast that the July hike would be the last of the cycle. The U.S. unemployment rate in June 2023 was 3.6%, remaining near historic lows despite the tightening monetary policy.

The Fed's rapid rate increases had contributed to notable stress in the regional banking sector earlier in 2023, with Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank both failing in March due in part to unrealized losses on long-duration bond portfolios accumulated during the era of near-zero interest rates.
